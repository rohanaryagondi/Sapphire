**Persona: Dr. Michael "Mike" Harrington, MD, PhD**
**Title:** EVP & CSO, Internal Medicine & Neuroscience
**Location:** New York, NY (global oversight US/EU/Asia)
**Experience:** 24+ years across academia, biotech, and large-cap pharma
**Reports To:** Chief R&D Officer
**Span of Control:** ~500 FTEs (Discovery, Translational Medicine, Early Clinical, Clinical Pharmacology, Data Sciences)

**1. Core Identity & Leadership DNA**
Dr. Harrington is a commercially pragmatic physician-scientist shaped by Pfizer's global scale and execution culture. His identity blends:
- **Science credibility**
- **Financial discipline**
- **Operational intensity**
- **Global mindset**
- **Speed-to-impact mentality**
He believes innovation must translate into **global commercial leadership**, not just scientific recognition.
He often says:
"If it can't scale globally, it's not a Pfizer program."

**2. Alignment with Pfizer Philosophy**
At Pfizer, Harrington operates under five institutional principles:

**1. Breakthroughs That Change Patients' Lives**
He prioritizes assets that:
- Address major unmet need
- Demonstrate clinically visible benefit
- Have potential for broad global uptake
- Justify premium reimbursement
Incremental differentiation is rarely sufficient.

**2. Capital Allocation Discipline**
Pfizer is rNPV-driven.
Harrington requires:
- Clear peak sales modeling early
- Probability-adjusted valuation
- Kill decisions at first sign of weak signal
- Resource reallocation to higher ROI assets
He expects every program to defend its capital annually.

**3. Scale & Commercial Leverage**
He evaluates programs through the lens of Pfizer's:
- Global commercial footprint
- Primary care reach
- Manufacturing infrastructure
- Global regulatory capability
He favors:
- Large patient populations
- Indications with global reimbursement pathways
- Programs expandable across regions

**4. External Innovation as Growth Engine**
Pfizer is acquisition-forward.
Harrington:
- Actively scouts clinical-stage assets
- Supports option-to-buy structures
- Prefers de-risked biology
- Is comfortable with large M&A when justified
He is less enthusiastic about:
- Early platform-only partnerships
- Long-cycle exploratory discovery

**5. Speed as Competitive Advantage**
Post-pandemic execution culture emphasizes:
- Parallel development tracks
- Rapid IND filing
- Global trial acceleration
- Early regulatory engagement
He measures teams on cycle time.

**3. Scientific Background**
- MD in Internal Medicine
- PhD in Molecular Pharmacology
- Former academic translational scientist
- Transitioned to industry to drive scalable impact
Deep expertise in:
- Clinical pharmacology
- Biomarker strategy
- Cardiometabolic & inflammation
- Select CNS exposure (neuropsychiatry, pain)
He is less of a "deep bench neuroscientist" and more of a translational execution leader.

**4. Strategic Priorities in CNS**
Pfizer's CNS exposure is selective.
Harrington prioritizes:
**1. High-Prevalence, Scalable Indications**
- Depression
- Anxiety
- Pain
- Neuropsychiatric disorders with large markets
Less emphasis on ultra-rare neurodegeneration unless platform enabling.

**2. Clinically Visible Differentiation**
He wants:
- Rapid onset
- Strong symptom improvement
- Clear safety edge
- Commercially meaningful benefit

**3. Risk Mitigation**
He aims to:
- Reduce Phase 2 failure
- Strengthen translational bridge
- Improve biomarker integration
But will not tolerate prolonged uncertain biology.

**5. Decision-Making Framework**
When evaluating a new program or partnership:

**Commercial Lens (First Filter)**
- Peak sales > $1-2B?
- Global reimbursement feasible?
- Competitive differentiation clear?
- IP durability strong?

**Scientific Lens**
- Human genetic support?
- Biomarker strategy defined?
- Target engagement measurable?
- Safety margin plausible?

**Execution Lens**
- Timeline to IND?
- Timeline to Phase 2?
- Cost to proof-of-concept?
- Manufacturing complexity?

**6. View on AI & Platforms**
Harrington views AI as:
- A productivity multiplier
- A cycle-time reducer
- A clinical optimization tool
He supports:
- Enterprise AI integration
- Trial simulation
- Predictive toxicology
- Target ranking acceleration
He is skeptical of:
- AI-first companies without asset clarity
- Platforms without near-term IND pathway
- Academic-scale discovery engines

**7. Pain Points & Frustrations**
- Late-stage trial failures
- Slow discovery cycles
- Programs that don't scale commercially
- Complex regulatory pathways
- Narrow indication strategies

**8. What Gets Him Excited**
- Technologies that shorten time to IND
- Functional human validation systems
- Tools that increase PoS meaningfully
- Scalable drug discovery platforms
- Data integration that reduces attrition
He is intrigued by:
- Large-scale human neuron functional maps
- Direct gene-to-drug comparability
- Systems that predict clinical effect size early
But only if tied to an asset path.

**9. How He Engages with Biotech**
**First Meeting**
- Focuses on commercial thesis
- Probes differentiation
**Second Meeting**
- Integrates translational team
- Reviews PoS modeling
**Third Meeting**
- Financial structuring
- Option-based deal modeling
He disengages if:
- The market is small
- The data are early without asset roadmap
- The pitch is overly academic

**10. Communication Style**
- Direct
- Financially literate
- Execution-focused
- Low tolerance for ambiguity
Prefers:
- Clear peak sales modeling
- Competitive positioning slides
- Development timeline clarity
- Quantitative PoS discussion

**11. Metrics He Tracks**
- rNPV by program
- Time from target ID to IND
- Phase 2 PoS
- Capital efficiency per asset
- Cycle time reduction
- Revenue concentration risk

**12. Long-Term Vision**
Harrington's ambition:
"Build a focused, scalable pipeline that wins globally."
He envisions:
- Capital-efficient R&D
- Faster development cycles
- Selective but high-impact CNS presence
- Strategic M&A as growth lever
- Enterprise-scale AI integration
