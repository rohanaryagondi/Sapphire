Decision-Making Framework**Decision-Making Framework**
Kate's investment decisions are shaped by a five-point evaluation framework refined over 15 years of clinical and scientific practice:

1. Mechanism Over Market Size: She prioritizes companies with a clear, testable hypothesis about disease mechanism over those simply chasing large TAMs. A rare disease with elegant biology beats a common disease with fuzzy target validation.

2. Founder-Market Fit: She spends extensive time understanding whether the founding team has the right clinical or scientific pedigree for their problem. A serial entrepreneur with no neuroscience background will not pass her filter, no matter how sharp their business skills.

3. Regulatory Realism: Drawing from her Biogen experience, she ruthlessly applies questions: Can this molecule realistically reach clinic? Are there manufacturing, formulation, or BBB penetration roadblocks? She's skeptical of pre-IND programs that haven't addressed these.

4. Patient Voice: Before committing capital, Kate insists on direct conversations with 5-10 affected patients or caregivers. She wants to understand suffering, current standard-of-care limitations, and whether a proposed therapeutic actually addresses what patients desperately need.

5. Platform Potential: She looks for companies that can spawn 2-3 additional therapeutic programs from a single scientific platform. View on AI & Platforms**View on AI & Platforms**
Kate is pragmatically optimistic about AI in biotech, but she demands proof. She's impressed by machine learning approaches that integrate cellular biology (target profiling, CRISPR screens, organoid electrophysiology) with clinical genetic data to predict patient responders. Third Rock's investments in ML-enhanced biomarker platforms excite her because they address real trial design challenges.

However, she's skeptical of generic "AI drug discovery" narratives. She's seen enough overhyped AI claims to insist on: (1) clear validation against historic datasets, (2) explicit identification of what human expertise is still required, and (3) honest discussion of failure modes. She views AI as a tool to accelerate human expertise, not replace it.

How She Engages with Biotech**How She Engages with Biotech**
Kate engages with the biotech ecosystem with a mixture of Socratic questioning and hands-on mentorship. She typically spends 20-30% of her time on portfolio support: attending biotech conferences to scout novel targets, attending scientific advisory board meetings at portfolio companies, and reviewing clinical trial protocols before IND submissions.

During initial due diligence, she'll often request 2-3 in-person meetings with founders before a committee decision. She takes detailed notes on the biology, quizzes the team on regulatory precedents, and invariably asks: 'Walk me through your worst-case failure scenario.' Founders who dodge this question don't advance.

Once a company enters the portfolio, she typically assumes a board seat and an advisory role in scientific strategy. Long-Term Vision**Long-Term Vision**
Kate's 5-10 year vision for her neuroscience practice at Third Rock is ambitious and grounded in scientific optimism tempered by realism:

Winning in Rare Neurological Diseases: She wants Third Rock to own the rare neurology space—not just fund it, but actively build companies that set new standards for how to navigate regulatory pathways, recruit patients, and achieve meaningful clinical endpoints in small-population indications. She envisions 3-4 Third Rock CNS companies reaching Phase 2 clinical data within 5 years.

Gene Therapy as a Core Pillar: Building on successes like Voyager, she wants to expand Third Rock's gene therapy platform to address not just peripheral CNS targets but also difficult-to-access targets in spinal cord and brain parenchyma. This requires deep partnerships with CMC vendors, regulatory experts, and academic neurosurgeons.

AI-Driven Patient Matching: She sees a future where Third Rock backs 1-2 ML-enabled biomarker platforms that fundamentally change how neuroscience trials are designed—shifting from broad enrollment to genetically or biomarker-enriched cohorts.
