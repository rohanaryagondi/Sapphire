## 5. DECISION-MAKING FRAMEWORK
Sheikh employs a data-first, patient-need-informed, commercially-savvy decision framework that balances science with business strategy.
Framework pillars:
- What is the unmet medical need? (clinical impact assessment)
- What is the mechanistic rationale? (scientific validity)
- What is the addressable market? (commercial opportunity)
- Can we differentiate meaningfully? (competitive advantage)
Example: When deciding whether to advance TAK-360 (dual OX1/OX2 antagonist) into Phase 2, Sheikh convened a task force to assess whether dual antagonism could address narcolepsy type 2 and hypersomnia better than selective OX2 agonism. After preclinical studies showed dual antagonism restored sleep architecture more effectively than selective approaches, she greenlit Phase 2.
"Every program must answer four questions: Is it needed? Is it sound? Can we win? Will shareholders value it? All four must be yes."
This rigor has shaped Takeda Neuroscience's portfolio: strategic but focused, ambitious but disciplined.
## 6. VIEW ON AI & PLATFORMS
Sheikh is pragmatically interested in AI for clinical operations and biomarker discovery, but skeptical of AI replacing clinical judgment in rare disease diagnosis.
AI applications Takeda Neuroscience explores:
- Sleep biomarker discovery: ML analysis of polysomnography (PSG) data to identify novel sleep architecture markers predictive of orexin deficiency or treatment response
- Patient phenotyping: Algorithm to match patient sleep disorder phenotypes to likely genetic causes (narcolepsy type 1 vs. type 2 vs. IH)
- Biomarker validation: AI to link hypocretin CSF levels (gold standard) to non-invasive markers (actigraphy, self-reported sleep metrics)
- Clinical trial optimization: Platforms to identify and recruit hard-to-reach patients with rare sleep disorders
Her stance: AI is a tool for efficiency and discovery, but does not replace neurologist clinical assessment and sleep medicine expertise.
Platform philosophy: Takeda partners with digital health vendors (sleep tracking wearables, remote monitoring) but maintains internal control over R&D strategy and regulatory decision-making. She views platforms as enablers of data generation, not decision-makers.
Vision: Within 3-5 years, Takeda's digital sleep ecosystem should enable real-world evidence generation in narcolepsy and hypersomnia cohorts—supporting post-approval studies and payer discussions.
## 7. PAIN POINTS
Despite TAK-861's potential, Sheikh confronts significant challenges:
- Patient awareness and diagnosis: Many narcolepsy and hypersomnia patients are undiagnosed or diagnosed late; market penetration is uncertain
- Physician education burden: Sleep medicine specialists are concentrated; educating primary care physicians requires sustained effort
- Payer reimbursement uncertainty: Insurers may require sleep studies or genetic testing before coverage; prior authorization complexity delays access
- Competitive landscape: Biohaven's GHB derivative (Xywav) dominates narcolepsy; displacing it requires clear superiority or convenience advantage
- Manufacturing scale-up: Scaling TAK-861 production to meet global demand while maintaining GMP compliance is operationally complex
- Rare neuro-genetic acquisitions: Target valuations are inflated; deal-making requires balancing shareholder ROI expectations with clinical mission
- Clinical heterogeneity: Narcolepsy phenotypes vary (cataplexic vs. non-cataplexic, early vs. late onset); demonstrating efficacy across subgroups is challenging
"Rare diseases are commercially harder than common diseases. We need both clinical conviction and shareholder patience."
