## 4. Core Scientific Focus: Vagal Neuro-Immune Signaling
Nilo's scientific foundation rests on decades of academic research into the vagus nerve's role in regulating immune responses. Dr. Kruidenier is deeply versed in this literature and its therapeutic implications.
The vagus nerve acts as a bidirectional communication highway between the brain and peripheral immune organs. Signals travel both afferently (from the periphery to the brain) and efferently (from the brain to immune tissues). Dysregulation of these pathways is implicated in numerous autoimmune and inflammatory conditions, from rheumatoid arthritis to inflammatory bowel disease to systemic lupus erythematosus.
Unlike conventional immunomodulatory approaches that globally suppress or activate immune cells, Nilo's platform targets the neuro-immune interface, aiming to restore balanced immune regulation through neural circuits. This approach offers potential advantages:
- Greater specificity: targeting neural circuits rather than broad immune populations
- Durability: potentially achieving disease-modifying effects by restoring immune homeostasis
- Favorable tolerability: leveraging endogenous biological mechanisms rather than foreign proteins or small molecules
- Accessibility: oral or minimally invasive delivery modalities compatible with chronic disease management
Dr. Kruidenier emphasizes that Nilo's programs are grounded in rigorous mechanistic neuroscience. The company does not simply modulate a receptor; it activates specific neural pathways that engage downstream immune tolerance mechanisms. This distinction is critical for designing rational clinical trials and anticipating real-world efficacy.
Key scientific principles guiding Nilo's strategy:
- Circuit-level specificity in neural targeting
- Functional immune readouts beyond biomarkers
- Longitudinal assessments of immune tolerance mechanisms
- Integration of human neuroscience data with preclinical models

## 7. Nilo's Preclinical Pipeline Strategy
Dr. Kruidenier oversees a thoughtfully sequenced preclinical pipeline designed to validate the neuro-immune platform across multiple disease indications and mechanistic approaches.
The pipeline is organized around several strategic axes:
Vagal Afferent Programs: These target sensory neurons within the vagal nodose ganglion, which detect immune signals from peripheral organs and relay them to the central nervous system. Modulating these pathways could enhance centralized immune tolerance mechanisms. Programs in this area are in advanced preclinical development.
Vagal Efferent Programs: These engage the parasympathetic nervous system's output to immune organs, including the spleen and mesenteric lymph nodes. The acetylcholine-α7 nicotinic receptor axis is a particular focus, given its well-characterized role in immune suppression.
Central Circuit Programs: These target higher-order brain regions that integrate neuro-immune signals and coordinate systemic responses. Such programs may offer broader immunomodulatory effects but come with increased complexity around CNS penetration and safety.
Dr. Kruidenier's strategic approach emphasizes:
- Rigorous target validation in human tissues and ex vivo immune assays
- Mechanism-of-action studies in physiologically relevant disease models
- Early assessment of potential off-target liabilities (particularly in autonomic function)
- Regulatory alignment on biomarker strategy and clinical trial design
- Intellectual property strategy to protect platform innovations
The preclinical timeline toward IND applications spans 18-24 months for lead programs, with later-stage programs following in 6-12 month intervals. This staged approach allows Nilo to gather human-relevant data and refine clinical development strategies before committing to expensive IND studies.
## 8. Disease Indications & Unmet Medical Need
Nilo's initial focus spans several autoimmune and inflammatory diseases with substantial unmet medical needs and large addressable markets.
Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA): Despite advances in TNF inhibitors and B-cell depleting therapies, 20-30% of RA patients fail to achieve remission. Moreover, long-term use of conventional immunosuppressants carries risks of infection and malignancy. A durable neuro-immune approach could address this treatment-resistant population.
Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD): Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis affect over 3 million people in the developed world. Current therapies focus on TNF inhibition, JAK inhibition, or S1P receptor modulation. None restore durable immune tolerance. The gut's direct vagal innervation makes it an ideal target for neuro-immune therapeutics.
Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE): With limited therapeutic options and significant morbidity from current immunosuppressants, SLE represents a high-value indication for a platform that could restore immune tolerance.
Beyond the initial three indications, the neuro-immune platform has potential applications in:
- Type 1 diabetes
- Psoriasis and psoriatic arthritis
- Multiple sclerosis
- Chronic infection (bacterial, viral, fungal)
- Sepsis and acute inflammatory responses
Dr. Kruidenier's indication strategy balances scientific tractability, commercial opportunity, and regulatory probability of success. The company's sequencing of indications reflects data emerging from preclinical programs.
Key unmet needs driving Nilo's strategy:
- Durable remission without long-term immunosuppression
- Restoration of immune tolerance rather than immune suppression
- Minimal impact on protective immunity (vaccination, infection control)
- Oral or minimally invasive administration
- No incremental infection or malignancy risk

## 11. Regulatory Strategy & Path to Clinic
Dr. Kruidenier is instrumental in crafting Nilo's regulatory strategy, which emphasizes early FDA engagement around novel mechanisms, biomarkers, and clinical trial design.
Key regulatory considerations:
Mechanism of Action (MOA): The FDA has limited experience with neuro-immune therapeutics targeting vagal circuits. Nilo's regulatory strategy involves detailed IND meetings to ensure FDA comfort with the proposed MOA, both before and early in clinical development.
Biomarkers: Rather than relying solely on clinical endpoints, Nilo is developing a robust biomarker strategy to demonstrate target engagement and mechanism engagement. Biomarkers may include:
- Vagal tone measures (heart rate variability)
- Immune cell subset frequencies and functional assays
- Inflammatory cytokine panels
- Neural imaging or electrophysiology (depending on program)
- Tissue-specific immune markers (gut lamina propria cells in IBD, synovial tissue in RA)
Animal Models: The transition from rodent models to larger species (non-human primates) will be important for establishing safety and dose ranges prior to IND filing.
Clinical Trial Design: For a first-in-human program, Nilo anticipates a Phase 1b PoC trial in a disease indication with robust, rapid clinical readouts. IBD is a prime candidate given the availability of endoscopic assessments.
Timeline to IND Applications:
- 2026: IND submissions for 2-3 lead programs
- 2027: Initiation of Phase 1b studies in lead indications
- 2028-2029: Advancement to Phase 2a in one or more indications based on Phase 1b data
Dr. Kruidenier's regulatory philosophy emphasizes transparency with the FDA, robust data generation, and realistic timelines. He understands that novel mechanisms require thorough preclinical validation and clear communication of the scientific rationale.
