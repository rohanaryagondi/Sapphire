# Dr. Nicholas Flytzanis, PhD
Founder & Chief Research/Innovation Officer, Capsida Biotherapeutics
## 1. CORE IDENTITY
Nick Flytzanis embodies the DNA engineer turned biotech entrepreneur—a synthetic biologist with genuine obsession for protein structure and molecular design. As founder and CRIO of Capsida, he channels his PhD passion for AAV capsid engineering into solving what many thought impossible: crossing the blood-brain barrier with engineered AAV vectors while maintaining transduction efficiency.
His core identity reflects:
"Rational, directed AAV capsid engineering beats hit-and-run screening."
Flytzanis believes that computational design, structural biology, and directed evolution (not just random mutagenesis) can unlock tissue-specific targeting and BBB penetration. This philosophy underpins every Capsida program.
Key identity markers: Deep biophysical thinker, patient with complexity, impatient with empiricism-only approaches, and committed to building a durable platform rather than one-hit wonders.
As founder-CRIO, Flytzanis leads R&D with scientific precision. He reviews crystal structures personally, debates codon optimization strategies with CMC teams, and makes all decisions through a structural biology lens. He is not a traditional "CEO" but rather a scientific visionary who happens to own equity.
## 2. ALIGNMENT WITH COMPANY PHILOSOPHY
Capsida's raison d'être is Flytzanis's engineering philosophy made manifest. He founded the company on the conviction that engineered AAV capsids could overcome the two biggest limitations of gene therapy: systemic biodistribution challenges and the blood-brain barrier.
Company philosophy crystallizes around:
- Directed AAV engineering over empirical screening: Use structural data, computational design, and rational mutagenesis—not just library screening—to generate superior capsids
- BBB penetration as a platform advantage: Engineer capsids that cross the BBB via transcytosis or adsorptive-mediated endocytosis; this opens neurological indications inaccessible to wild-type AAV
- Organ de-targeting: Simultaneously engineer liver and dorsal root ganglia off-targeting to reduce peripheral toxicity and redirect payload to CNS
- Scalable manufacturing: Design for CMC manufacturability from the outset; avoid capsids that work biochemically but fail in GMP settings
Daily operations align tightly: CAP-002 for STXBP1-DEE (developmental and epileptic encephalopathy) exemplifies the platform—an engineered capsid, IV administration, >70% neuronal transduction, minimal liver uptake, FDA Fast Track designation. This validates Flytzanis's thesis at regulatory and clinical levels.

## 5. DECISION-MAKING FRAMEWORK
Flytzanis employs a structure-driven, mechanism-first decision framework that would be familiar to synthetic biologists.
Framework pillars:
- Is the engineering rationally justified? (structural biology lens)
- Can we manufacture it reproducibly? (CMC feasibility)
- Does it address a true mechanistic bottleneck? (biological impact)
- Is the preclinical signal robust and replicable? (statistical rigor)
Example: When considering whether to pursue PD-GBA (Parkinson's with GBA mutations), Flytzanis convened a 4-person task force to model BBB penetration requirements, estimate enzyme activity needed, and review GBA biochemistry literature. Only after structural modeling and a designed experiment showing >70% neuronal transduction in mouse cortex did he greenlight the program.
"If we can't explain why it works at the molecular level, it's not a platform—it's luck."
This rigor has shaped Capsida's culture: scientists learn to defend every claim with structural or mechanistic data.
## 6. VIEW ON AI & PLATFORMS
Flytzanis is enthusiastic about AI for structure prediction and capsid design—areas where computational tools directly enhance his core thesis.
AI applications Capsida leverages:
- AlphaFold2 for capsid structure modeling and variant predictions
- Machine learning for transduction prediction (genotype -> functional outcome)
- High-throughput sequencing data analysis to identify functional variants from directed evolution screens
- Natural language processing for patent landscaping and competitive intelligence
His enthusiasm is nuanced: AI augments but doesn't replace wet-lab validation. He rejected a proposal to use ML to predict clinical efficacy from preclinical data ("correlation is not causation; we need mechanism").
Platform philosophy: Capsida partners with CROs for manufacturing scale-up and regulatory consulting, but retains all R&D and scientific strategy internally. Flytzanis is adamant that outsourcing core innovation is antithetical to maintaining competitive advantage.
Vision: Within 2-3 years, Capsida aims to field 5+ program-specific engineered capsids and license the platform to pharma partners for additional indications. The goal is to become the "architecture layer" of gene therapy.
## 7. PAIN POINTS
Despite engineering elegance, Flytzanis confronts real challenges:
- Immunogenicity of engineered capsids: Even optimized variants can trigger innate immunity in some patients; residual hepatotoxicity or transient inflammation remain unpredictable
- Manufacturing scale-up complexity: Engineered capsids work well in small batches but GMP manufacturing requires re-validation; yield and consistency variability frustrates him
- Preclinical-to-clinical translation gaps: Strong mouse model data doesn't always translate to non-human primates; NHP studies are expensive and logistically difficult
- Regulatory ambiguity: The FDA hasn't articulated clear guidance on engineered AAV capsids; each program may require novel regulatory justification
- Patient recruitment in ultra-rare indication: STXBP1-DEE has ~200 global patients; finding and enrolling them while ensuring natural history control data is a logistical nightmare
- Intellectual property landscape: AAV capsid patents are crowded; Capsida must design around existing IP or license in, which dilutes margins
"Engineering is hard; biology is harder. Combining them in a clinical setting is brutal."

## 11. METRICS THEY TRACK
Flytzanis maintains a personal R&D dashboard that merges scientific and commercial metrics.
Scientific metrics:
- Transduction efficiency (% neurons, % by brain region)
- Hepatic uptake ratio (target tissue vs. liver, expressed as fold-change)
- DRG (dorsal root ganglia) off-targeting: % dorsal root ganglia neurons transduced (should be <10% if targeting CNS)
- Innate immune activation markers (IL-6, TNF-alpha, interferon response) at transduction-equivalent doses
- Durability of transduction: % gene expression retained at weeks 4, 8, 12 post-injection
Preclinical & clinical metrics:
- CAP-002 IND readiness: % of regulatory documents completed
- STXBP1-DEE patient identification and enrollment rate (target: 10-15 per site)
- Natural history cohort recruitment and biomarker acquisition (seizure frequency, EEG, MRI, blood biomarkers)
Business metrics:
- Partnership revenue (AbbVie upfront, milestones, royalties)
- Manufacturing yields at scale
- Cost-per-dose trajectory
- Runway and capital efficiency
Flytzanis reviews these weekly. Monthly R&D meetings begin with a metrics deep-dive, ensuring every team member understands performance against targets.
