# Dr. Kevin Eggan, PhD
Co-Founder & Chief Scientific Advisor, QurAlis
## 1. CORE IDENTITY
Kevin Eggan is a MacArthur Fellow and Harvard stem cell biologist turned ALS therapeutics entrepreneur. His identity is rooted in patient-derived cellular models and precision medicine—the belief that patient-specific stem cells, differentiated into disease-relevant cell types, can unlock mechanistic understanding and enable targeted therapy screening.
His core thesis:
"Patient cells are the best bioassay. Use them to understand disease, identify precision targets, and validate compounds in a physiologically relevant context."
Eggan co-founded QurAlis to translate this philosophy into clinical reality. He is obsessed with ALS pathophysiology—particularly the protein STATHMIN-2 in splicing and neurodegeneration—and sees ALS/FTD as the ideal model for precision medicine in neurodegeneration.
Key identity markers: Thoughtful, academically rigorous, patient-centric, skeptical of reductionist disease models, and committed to validating targets through human cellular systems before advancing to clinic.
As Co-Founder and CSA, Eggan does not manage day-to-day operations but serves as scientific north star. He spends ~50% time at Harvard, 50% at QurAlis, maintaining academic rigor while driving commercial progress.
## 2. ALIGNMENT WITH COMPANY PHILOSOPHY
QurAlis exists to operationalize Eggan's patient-cell paradigm. The company's entire strategy rests on converting patient-derived induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs) into motor neurons, astrocytes, and other disease-relevant cell types to identify and validate therapeutic targets.
Company philosophy comprises three pillars:
- Patient cell primacy: Derive iPSCs from ALS and FTD patients, differentiate into relevant cell types, and use them to understand disease and identify targets
- Precision genetics focus: Concentrate on genetically-defined ALS/FTD subgroups (SOD1, C9orf72, GRN, STXBP1, etc.) where target validation is most tractable
- Modular therapeutic modalities: Exploit multiple platforms (antisense, ASO, RNA interference, small molecule) to target the same gene/pathway; choose modality based on tissue biology and patient needs
- Rigorous target validation: Only advance targets that show consistent signal across multiple patient iPSC-derived cell types and independent replication studies
QRL-201 (STATHMIN-2 antisense) exemplifies this philosophy: discovered through patient motor neuron screens, validated across multiple patient genotypes, and now in IND-enabling studies. Eggan insisted on deriving motor neurons from 8+ independent patient lines before committing to clinical development.

## 5. DECISION-MAKING FRAMEWORK
Eggan employs a patient-cell-first, rigorous-validation framework that is both mission-driven and scientifically stringent.
Framework pillars:
- Is the signal credible in patient iPSC-motor neurons? (biological validation)
- Is there mechanistic clarity? (pathway understanding)
- Can we measure it in patients? (translatable biomarkers)
- Is the modality appropriate to the target and biology? (drug design fit)
Example: When considering UNC13A as a target, Eggan commissioned a 6-month study examining UNC13A expression, splicing variants, and functional rescue in motor neurons from 20+ ALS patients. Only after seeing consistent signal did he greenlight the program.
"We move when biology is clear, not when hope is high."
This patience is unusual in biotech, where timelines are typically aggressive. But Eggan's discipline has built QurAlis's credibility with the ALS community and regulators.
## 6. VIEW ON AI & PLATFORMS
Eggan is pragmatic about AI—appreciative of its potential for image analysis and target discovery, but skeptical of AI replacing human cellular biology.
AI applications QurAlis explores:
- High-content imaging analysis: AI to quantify motor neuron morphology, neurite outgrowth, and cell viability from 100,000+ images
- Biomarker prediction: Machine learning to link iPSC phenotypes (gene expression, protein levels) to patient outcomes
- Compound screening: AI-assisted hit identification from large chemical libraries
- Patient matching for clinical trials: Algorithm to identify genetically-stratified patients most likely to benefit
His stance: AI is a tool for speed and efficiency, not ground truth. Patient cells and human biology remain the final arbiter of relevance.
Platform philosophy: QurAlis maintains proprietary iPSC banks (from 200+ ALS patients) and cell differentiation protocols. It partners with CROs for clinical supply chain and regulatory consulting but retains all scientific and target discovery internally.
Vision: Within 5 years, QurAlis envisions the largest patient iPSC biobank linked to genomic data and clinical outcomes—a "living library" of ALS biology.
## 7. PAIN POINTS
Despite the elegance of patient-cell modeling, Eggan faces endemic challenges:
- iPSC variability: Patient-derived iPSCs exhibit substantial clonal and line-to-line variation; ensuring reproducible differentiation requires extensive optimization
- Maturation bottleneck: In vitro motor neurons don't fully recapitulate adult physiology; extrapolating from immature cell models to human adult disease is mechanistically uncertain
- Target translation gap: A compelling in vitro signal (e.g., 30% improvement in motor neuron survival) often translates to modest clinical benefit; bridging this gap frustrates him
- ALS heterogeneity: Even genetically-stratified patients show variable progression and response. Precision medicine in ALS is more art than science
- Regulatory uncertainty: The FDA hasn't clearly endorsed iPSC-based target validation; QurAlis must educate regulators on cell-based biomarker relevance
- Manufacturing scale: Growing and differentiating iPSCs at commercial scale while maintaining quality is complex and costly
"Patient cells are beautiful for discovery. Translation to clinic is where dreams meet harsh reality."

Long-term excitement: Eggan dreams of a future where precision medicine ALS treatment is routine. Patients are genetically stratified, their cells are cultured to understand disease, and targeted therapies are matched to genotype and phenotype.
"In 15 years, every ALS patient should have iPSC-derived motor neurons characterized in a research lab guiding their treatment."
