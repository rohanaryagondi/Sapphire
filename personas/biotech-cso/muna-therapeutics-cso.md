Dr. Rita Balice-Gordon, PhD
CEO, Muna Therapeutics (Copenhagen)
## 1. Role & Scope
Dr. Balice-Gordon is the founding CEO of Muna Therapeutics, a Copenhagen-based neuroscience company focused on neuronal resilience as a therapeutic principle for neurodegenerative diseases. Her role encompasses all scientific, strategic, clinical, and business functions, reporting to the Board and managing investor relationships, academic collaborations, and regulatory interactions. She co-founded Muna with Simon Glerup (Aarhus University) to translate mechanistic discoveries about neuronal resilience into first-in-class therapeutic interventions. Muna's platform leverages the progranulin pathway (critical for neuronal health and resistance to stress), spatial transcriptomics, and AI computational chemistry to identify and advance candidates targeting neuronal resilience in FTD (frontotemporal dementia) and Alzheimer's disease. The company's €33.5M GSK partnership and recent $73M Series A funding validate her vision and position Muna as a Europe-based neurobiotech leader.
Key Responsibilities:
- Platform development: Progranulin pathway validation and neuronal resilience mechanism
- Clinical program advancement: FTD and AD candidate development and regulatory strategy
- Strategic partnerships: GSK collaboration, academic relationships (Aarhus, broader EU)
- Technology integration: Spatial transcriptomics + AI computational chemistry platform
## 2. Strategic Priorities
Dr. Balice-Gordon's strategic vision is to establish neuronal resilience as a validated therapeutic principle and Muna as its clinical exemplar. FTD, particularly progranulin (GRN) mutation-associated FTD, is the validation indication; the "all-in-human" approach (target discovery directly from human biology) ensures translational rigor. The GSK partnership provides commercial scale, regulatory expertise, and geographic reach; optimizing this collaboration while maintaining Muna's scientific independence is critical. The $73M Series A enables advancement of FTD programs toward IND and early clinical validation. Her strategic priorities include: (1) demonstrating progranulin pathway engagement in human tissues and disease models, (2) advancing lead candidates through IND-enabling studies, (3) establishing Muna's spatial transcriptomics + AI platform as differentiated, and (4) positioning Muna as a Europe-based leader in neurodegenerative disease innovation. Long-term, she sees Muna as a multi-indication neurodegeneration company with a proprietary platform generating multiple clinical candidates.
Immediate Objectives:
- Validate progranulin pathway engagement and neuronal resilience mechanisms in human models
- Advance FTD lead candidate through IND-enabling toxicology and PK studies
- Optimize GSK partnership and establish governance frameworks
- Expand microglial programs and validate immune resilience as therapeutic axis

## 4. Key Challenges & Pain Points
Muna faces significant challenges translating neuronal resilience from concept to clinic. FTD is a small patient population (estimated 60K cases in US); clinical trial recruitment and retention are notoriously difficult. Progranulin biology, while mechanistically compelling, is incompletely understood in human disease—many questions remain about optimal target engagement and biomarker qualification. Spatial transcriptomics, though cutting-edge, has limited clinical translation precedent; demonstrating relevance for patient stratification and therapy selection is ambitious. The GSK partnership, while valuable, also introduces dependency and governance complexity. Competition from larger pharma (Eli Lilly, Roche, Biogen) investing heavily in FTD and broader neurodegeneration is formidable. Regulatory expectations for rare diseases like FTD are increasingly stringent; adaptive trial designs and patient-centric approaches are required. EU regulatory pathways differ from US; coordinating global development is complex. The "all-in-human" philosophy, while scientifically rigorous, sacrifices some preclinical validation, raising execution risk.
Critical Pressure Points:
- FTD patient recruitment and trial site activation in rare disease space
- Progranulin pathway engagement qualification and biomarker validation
- Spatial transcriptomics clinical translation and patient stratification utility
- GSK partnership complexity and maintenance of scientific independence

## 6. Market Understanding & Industry Perspective
Dr. Balice-Gordon views the neurodegeneration space as bifurcated between large-market Alzheimer's and Parkinson's programs (pursued by major pharma) and underserved rare neurodegenerative diseases like FTD. She sees FTD as a "best-of-both-worlds" indication: small enough to allow rapid trial execution and regulatory flexibility, yet large enough (and with clear unmet medical need) to support a viable commercial program. She recognizes that rare disease biotech increasingly attracts investor interest, particularly if backed by mechanistic insight and translational rigor. The neuronal resilience concept is novel and defensible; if validated, it could enable platform expansion into AD, PD, ALS, and other indications. Europe, particularly Copenhagen and Scandinavia, has become a neurobiotech hub; Muna's Copenhagen base positions it well within that ecosystem. The GSK partnership validates Muna's science and provides market access; she anticipates that successful early data could accelerate commercialization or attract additional pharma partnerships.
Market Observations:
- FTD remains one of the most underserved rare neurodegenerative diseases
- Neuronal resilience as therapeutic principle is novel and defensible
- European biotech ecosystem (Copenhagen, Scandinavia) is thriving
- Rare disease biotech with mechanistic validation attracts institutional interest
