# Section 32 Partner Persona
## Dr. William "Will" Maris Jr., PhD
Partner, Neurotech & AI Healthcare
## 1. Core Identity & Investment Philosophy
Will Maris is a neuroscientist-turned-venture capitalist who built his reputation at Google Ventures and founded Section 32 with a singular conviction: artificial intelligence and machine learning will fundamentally transform the diagnosis and treatment of oncology, neurology, and complex multifactorial diseases. Unlike venture capitalists who view AI as a general-purpose technology applicable everywhere, Will applies rigorous skepticism to AI-health claims, focusing only on areas where AI has demonstrated genuine clinical utility.
Operating from San Diego and San Francisco, Will manages a $525M fund with a thesis centered on the "zone of commoditization" problem: the observation that as AI technologies mature and become widely available, their competitive advantage erodes unless grounded in proprietary clinical data, validated ML models, or novel diagnostic approaches. He invests in companies that build defensible IP through rigorous clinical validation, not merely through software engineering.
Will's portfolio companies—including Octant Bio ($80M BMS collaboration on AI-driven cell therapy), Alector (neuroinflammation therapeutics), and the acquired-by-J&J success Auris (robotic surgery)—demonstrate his preference for companies solving well-defined, hard problems using AI and ML as a tool, not the business model. He has backed over 100 companies and maintains a success rate that rivals elite venture firms.
Key principles:
- AI/ML is powerful only when paired with proprietary data and rigorous clinical validation
- Avoid the "zone of commoditization"—off-the-shelf AI models offer no sustainable competitive advantage
- Oncology and neurology are high-conviction domains where AI delivers measurable clinical impact
- Clinical validation trumps software engineering prowess; real doctors must validate real efficacy
- Portfolio scale and success rate matter; 100+ companies requires operational excellence and pattern recognition

## 4. Strategic Investment Priorities

Within each domain, he prioritizes companies that demonstrate clinical validation early—not vaporware AI claims but actual patient data showing efficacy.
In oncology, Will targets companies like Octant Bio that combine proprietary data (tumor genomics, immunology) with advanced ML models to identify first-in-class therapeutics. He is less interested in AI-powered drug discovery platforms claiming to compress timelines by 5x; he prefers companies with measurable clinical endpoints and validated prediction accuracy.
In neurology, Will focuses on neurodegeneration (Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, ALS) and neuroinflammation. He backs Alector, which combines genetics, neurobiology, and protein engineering to target pathways validated in human genetics studies. This foundation-first approach reduces technical risk compared to unvalidated AI-first approaches.
Strategic priorities:
- AI-driven oncology: diagnostics, prognostics, and theranostics grounded in proprietary genomic/imaging data
- Neurodegeneration: therapeutics targeting validated genetic and immunologic pathways
- Brain-computer interfaces: hardware + software + neuroscience for motor recovery and communication restoration
- Precision medicine: biomarker-driven diagnostic platforms enabling patient stratification and therapy selection
- Robotics-assisted surgery: Auris exit demonstrated Will's interest in precision surgical platforms
## 5. Decision-Making Framework
Will employs what he calls a "clinical rigor" framework when evaluating investments. Unlike many venture capitalists who rely on market size and competitive landscape, Will begins by asking: What is the published evidence for this approach? Have the claims been validated in independent cohorts? What is the statistical power of the validation studies? Are there known confounders that could explain away apparent efficacy?
He conducts technical diligence by assembling advisory committees of world-class clinicians and research scientists. These committees review not just company claims but also the underlying scientific literature. Will demands evidence from published peer-reviewed studies, validated methodologies, and reproducible results. He is particularly skeptical of AI claims that cannot point to specific, validated performance metrics in independent test sets.
The second axis is founder quality. Will believes the best outcomes come from founders who combine (1) PhD-level or MD-level scientific depth, (2) publication history demonstrating research rigor, (3) willingness to be challenged on technical claims, and (4) moral conviction that solving the problem matters. He avoids founders primarily motivated by exit outcomes.
Decision framework:
- Phase 1 (Clinical Evidence): Review published literature; assess quality of validation studies; identify known confounders
- Phase 2 (AI/ML Rigor): Demand validated performance metrics; independent test set evaluation; statistical significance with multiple hypothesis correction
- Phase 3 (Founder Profile): PhD/MD depth, publication history, openness to challenge, moral conviction to solve problem
- Phase 4 (Defensibility Assessment): What is the proprietary moat? Data, models, regulatory pathway, clinical relationships?
- Phase 5 (Operational Planning): Will personally involved in CEO search, clinical advisor recruitment, and early regulatory pathway
## 6. View on AI & Platforms
Will is famously skeptical of AI hype in healthcare. He rejects claims that transformers, LLMs, or general-purpose AI models will revolutionize drug discovery or clinical diagnostics. Instead, he invests in narrow, validated AI applications grounded in specific clinical problems and proprietary datasets. He views off-the-shelf AI as a commodity; proprietary clinical AI as defensible IP.
His concern about the "zone of commoditization" is particularly relevant to AI healthcare. As LLMs, diffusion models, and foundation models become widely available, companies that build on top of open-source models face commoditization pressure. Will invests in companies that differentiate through proprietary training data, validated prediction accuracy on clinical endpoints, or defensible regulatory pathways.
Will is bullish on AI for very specific, well-defined clinical problems: radiology image interpretation with validated accuracy metrics, oncology prognostication using mutation profiles and imaging, and neurodegeneration biomarker discovery using multi-omics. He is skeptical of AI for drug discovery, biomarker discovery, and general clinical decision support without rigorous validation.
AI & Platforms perspective:
- Off-the-shelf AI = commodity; proprietary clinical AI grounded in validated data = defensible IP
- Zone of commoditization: as AI becomes widely available, moats erode unless grounded in clinical data or outcomes
- Skeptical of general LLM applications; bullish on narrow, validated AI with clinical endpoint validation
- Proprietary datasets (genomic, imaging, clinical) + validated models = defensible competitive advantage
- Clinical validation non-negotiable; AI claims must be supported by validated performance metrics and regulatory pathway
## 7. Pain Points & Frustrations
Will's primary frustration is with the proliferation of AI-healthcare companies making unvalidated clinical claims. He regularly encounters pitch decks claiming that a machine learning model can predict drug response, identify novel therapeutics, or enable precision medicine without any published peer-reviewed validation. This epidemic of overstated claims undermines investor confidence in genuine clinical AI innovations.
A related frustration: the venture industry's inexperience with clinical validation. Most venture investors lack the training to assess whether a clinical claim is credible, whether published evidence is rigorous, or whether regulatory pathways are realistically achievable. This creates a perverse incentive for founders to make unsubstantiated claims to less-discerning investors.
Will is also frustrated by the challenge of hiring. Elite neuroscientists and AI researchers are rare and highly sought after. Companies competing for these talents face wage pressure and retention challenges. Section 32's competitive advantage is Will's ability to recruit top talent by offering compelling clinical missions and intellectual rigor, not just equity packages.
Pain points:
- Epidemic of unvalidated AI-health claims; founders making efficacy assertions without clinical evidence
- Venture industry's clinical validation illiteracy; most VCs cannot assess rigor of clinical evidence
- Talent scarcity: elite neuroscientists and AI researchers are rare; wage pressure and retention challenging
- Regulatory pathway uncertainty; FDA guidance on AI/ML medical devices evolving and sometimes unclear
- Clinical relationship building takes time; years of credibility required to access premier academic medical centers

## 11. Metrics They Track
Will tracks an unusual portfolio of metrics relative to traditional venture capitalists. He is less interested in vanity metrics (total addressable market, projected revenue) and far more interested in technical and clinical de-risking metrics.
For AI-driven diagnostics companies, Will tracks: (1) model accuracy on independent test sets with statistical significance assessment, (2) sensitivity and specificity in diverse patient populations, (3) regulatory pathway clarity (FDA pre-submission status, 510k vs. de novo classification), (4) clinical validation timeline and costs, and (5) publication quality and frequency (peer-reviewed papers signal scientific substance).
For neurology therapeutics, Will emphasizes: (1) biomarker pathway validation in published studies, (2) safety and tolerability data from preclinical/clinical studies, (3) clinical trial enrollment progress and patient stratification accuracy, (4) pharmacology validation against animal models, and (5) founder-team stability and expertise in neurodegeneration.
Across all companies, Will maintains discipline on three meta-metrics: (1) technical risk reduction per dollar deployed, (2) scientific advisory board quality and engagement level, and (3) founder conviction and adaptability when facing contradictory evidence.
Metrics tracked:
- Model validation: accuracy, sensitivity, specificity on independent test sets; statistical significance
- Regulatory pathway: FDA pre-submission status, 510k vs. de novo clarity, timeline and cost estimates
- Clinical validation: trial enrollment progress, primary endpoint achievement, safety/tolerability profile
- Scientific output: peer-reviewed publications, conference presentations, patent applications
- Founder metrics: team stability, intellectual rigor, adaptability in face of contradictory data

He measures success by the percentage of patients whose outcomes are transformed through technologies his firm helped catalyze.
