# Alnylam Pharmaceuticals: SVP, Corporate Development & Strategy
## Persona: Dr. Evan Rourke, PhD, MBA
Location: Cambridge, MA
## 1. Core Identity & BD Philosophy
- Platform company in transition from 'build it all internally' to 'selective external innovation'
- Well-capitalized with $2.8B in cash reserves; recently achieved profitability in 2025
- Alnylam 2030 strategy targets ~30% of revenues reinvested into R&D (including external innovation)
- Disciplined scaling approach with RNAi-centric platform but open to complementary delivery technologies
- NOT pursuing major M&A; instead focused on selective licensing and strategic partnerships
## 2. Alignment with Alnylam BD Strategy
- Co-development/co-commercialization model exemplified by Roche partnership (50/50 U.S. profit share, $310M upfront)
- Platform licensing with equity components (Regeneron: $400M equity + discovery funding model)
- Royalty-bearing outbound licenses to scale reach (partnerships with Sanofi, Novartis established)
- 2-2-5 tissue expansion goals: 2 adipose programs, 2 muscle programs, 5 additional tissue targets (heart, kidney, CNS leadership)
- First profitable year achieved in 2025; now balancing growth investment with shareholder returns
## 3. Professional Background
- PhD in molecular biology with pharmacology specialization; MBA from Wharton/Harvard/MIT
- 15+ years of BD and licensing experience at top-tier pharma (Takeda, AstraZeneca, Novartis)
- Deep expertise in platform licensing economics and valuation of RNAi technology portfolios
- Extensive experience structuring complex co-development and co-commercialization agreements
- Track record negotiating $200M-$1B+ deals; comfortable with sophisticated risk-sharing models
## 4. Strategic BD Priorities
- CNS delivery leadership: ALN-APP (Alzheimer's) and ALN-HTT02 (Huntington's) via Regeneron partnership
- Tissue expansion beyond liver: adipose, muscle, heart, kidney programs in development
- Metabolic and obesity space expansion (ALN-2232 for APOC3, ALN-4324 for obesity programs)
- Selective in-licensing of complementary delivery technologies that extend RNAi platform capability
- Strategic out-licensing to generate upfront capital and fund high-priority internal programs
## 5. Decision-Making Framework
- Scientific fit: Genetic validation of target via Paradigm4 genomic data; peer-reviewed evidence required
- Risk/reward balance: Partnerships for high-risk, high-reward programs; internal development for validated programs
- Strategic fit with 2-2-5 tissue expansion goals; prioritizes programs addressing unmet medical needs
- Financial discipline: Milestone affordability, ROI expectations vs. 30% margin targets, cash runway impact
- Integration complexity: Prefers partnerships that leverage existing infrastructure; low diversion from core focus
- Platform synergy: Technologies that extend RNAi to new tissues or improve delivery get priority scoring
## 6. View on Deal Structures & Partnerships
- Prefers co-development with large pharma partners for blockbuster-potential programs; shared risk model
- $300M-$1B upfront capital typical for major outbound licensing; milestone-driven with cost-sharing
- Avoids high back-loaded royalty structures (views Sanofi 30% escalation deal as cautionary tale)
- Values equity stakes in partners as long-term alignment mechanism and strategic signaling
- Upfront capital focus for funding internal expansion; will out-license to maintain financial flexibility
- Non-exclusive vs. exclusive licensing: Non-exclusive preferred for platform tech; exclusive for specific programs
## 7. Pain Points & Frustrations
- Sanofi deal's high 30% royalty escalation serves as cautionary example of over-committed economics
- Partner opt-out risk: Regeneron's potential exercise of platform opt-out threatens ALN-APP program
- RNAi-only strategy perception limiting partnership interest from companies seeking diverse modality access
- Liver-centric platform perception remains challenge despite tissue expansion efforts and data generation
- Difficulty proving value of platform licensing vs. single asset licensing in partner discussions
- Intense competition for CNS delivery solutions space; multiple modalities pursuing brain penetration
## 8. What Gets Them Excited
- Novel tissue delivery technologies enabling extrahepatic RNAi applications; scientifically validated approaches
- CNS-penetrating delivery platforms that overcome blood-brain barrier challenges for genetic medicines
- Genetic validation of new disease targets via population genetics data; GWAS-identified validation compelling
- Co-development structures with shared economics and aligned incentives; mutual upside participation
- Technologies extending RNAi applicability to muscle, CNS, or difficult-to-transfect tissues
- Data demonstrating clinical translation of genetically validated targets; proof-of-concept in humans preferred
## 9. How They Engage with Biotech Partners
- First meeting: Rapid assessment of platform synergy and RNAi relevance; 20-30 minute filter conversation
- Second meeting: Scientific deep-dive with CSO Kevin Sloan; presentation of mechanism, preclinical data, target validation
- Third meeting: Deal economics and structure discussion; financial modeling and milestone scenarios
- Values long-term partnerships over transactional deals; prefers multi-program relationships with strategic depth
- Careful evaluation of exclusive vs. non-exclusive access; protective of core platform but pragmatic on specific programs
- Slow, methodical process: expects 2-3 month evaluation cycle before decision; no rushing to close
## 10. Communication & Negotiation Style
- Scientifically sophisticated: Speaks in detail about mechanisms, targets, and delivery science; no oversimplification
- Platform-first thinking: Evaluates every opportunity through lens of extending RNAi platform capability
- Disciplined on economics: Drives hard bargains on pricing; uses benchmarks and precedent deals as anchors
- Patient negotiator with no artificial rush: Can walk away cleanly if deal doesn't fit financial parameters
- Values scientific credibility of partner team: Questions unpublished claims; prefers peer-reviewed validation
- Prefers data-driven discussions: Bring clear evidence, regulatory pathways, and clinical development rationale
- Will walk away from deals that don't fit platform strategy; respects his time and won't pursue poor cultural fit
## 11. Metrics They Track
- Platform expansion milestones: Programs launched in new tissue targets vs. 2-2-5 goal
- Partnership revenue contribution: % of annual revenue from co-development, co-commercialization partners
- R&D spend vs. 30% target: Annual tracking against Alnylam 2030 strategic goal
- Milestone achievement rates: % of earned partnership milestones on pace vs. internal forecasts
- CNS program progression: Regulatory and clinical progress on ALN-APP, ALN-HTT02 partnerships
- Out-licensing royalty income: Annual revenue generated from platform and program licensing deals
- Cash runway: Months of operational cash on hand; important given transition to profitability phase
- Operating margin trajectory: Path to sustainable profitability at 30%+ margins by 2030
## 12. Long-Term BD Vision
- Transform Alnylam from liver-focused RNAi company to multi-tissue genetic medicines leader by 2030
- Build selective partnership ecosystem with large pharma for co-development and co-commercialization
- Achieve $4B+ revenue by 2030 through combination of internal product growth and partnered program contributions
- Establish RNAi as dominant genetic medicine modality for monogenic and complex disease targets
- Enable CNS breakthroughs through delivery innovation partnerships; position Alnylam as RNAi leader in brain diseases
- Maintain capital discipline and selective M&A approach while maximizing partnership value creation
