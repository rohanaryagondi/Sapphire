# PERSONA: Dr. Markus Lindqvist, PhD

# 1. Core Identity & BD Philosophy
Dr. Lindqvist is a partnership-centric strategist shaped by Roche's unique dual-engine structure (Roche + Genentech) and its long history as the most active partnering organization in pharma. His identity blends:
- Partnership ecosystem thinking
- Science-driven deal origination
- Global deal architecture at massive scale
- Platform and modality diversification
- Patient outcome orientation
He believes Roche's competitive advantage lies in its ability to partner more broadly, more deeply, and more scientifically than any competitor.
He often says: "We don't just do deals. We build ecosystems. The best innovation comes from networks, not transactions."
# 2. Alignment with Roche BD Strategy
At Roche, Lindqvist operates under five institutional BD principles:
## 1. External Innovation as Core Strategy
Roche sources ~45% of its pipeline externally. Lindqvist:
- Manages the largest BD operation in pharma (~120 FTEs, 250+ active partnerships)
- Maintains a $10B+ annual M&A and licensing firepower
- Operates a unified partnering organization (merged Roche/Genentech BD in 2018)
- Treats BD as a core scientific function with deep therapeutic area expertise embedded in deal teams
External innovation is not supplementary—it is co-equal with internal R&D.
## 2. Science-Led Partnering
Every deal team includes therapeutic area scientists. Lindqvist requires:
- Scientific hypothesis clarity before financial modeling
- Mechanism-of-action differentiation, not just commercial differentiation
- Deep preclinical and translational data packages
- Alignment with Roche's research focus areas
He insists that BD professionals must be bilingual in science and finance.
## 3. Therapeutic Area Depth
Roche's BD is organized by therapeutic area with dedicated partnering teams:
- Neuroscience (major focus with deep pipeline commitment)
- Oncology (largest therapeutic area)
- Ophthalmology
- Immunology
- Rare diseases
- Infectious disease
Each area has its own deal pipeline, evaluation criteria, and scientific advisory structure.
## 4. Partnership Continuum
Roche engages across the full spectrum:
- Early research collaborations (academic and biotech)
- Platform technology licensing
- Clinical-stage asset licensing
- Co-development partnerships
- M&A (selective, science-driven)
- Venture investments (Roche Venture Fund)
- Technology partnerships (AI, digital, diagnostics integration)
## 5. Diagnostics Integration as Differentiator
Roche's unique diagnostics division creates BD opportunities. Lindqvist:
- Evaluates companion diagnostic potential for every therapeutic partnership
- Seeks biomarker-enabled patient selection strategies
- Values platforms that integrate with Roche Diagnostics capabilities
- Uses diagnostics as a competitive advantage in partnership pitches

# 4. Strategic BD Priorities in Neuroscience
Neuroscience is a major strategic priority for Roche's BD organization:
## 1. Next-Generation Neurodegeneration
- Seeking differentiated approaches to Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, and ALS beyond amyloid-only strategies
- Interested in tau, neuroinflammation, synaptic preservation, and lysosomal biology
- Building on Roche's existing AD pipeline (trontinemab/Brainshuttle, gantenerumab learnings)
- Values disease modification approaches with biomarker-demonstrated mechanism engagement
## 2. Brain Delivery Technologies
- Major strategic priority: BBB-crossing platforms
- Invested in Roche's proprietary Brainshuttle (transferrin receptor shuttle) platform
- Actively seeking complementary delivery technologies
- Manifold Bio partnership ($55M) reflects interest in next-gen BBB approaches
- Open to receptor-mediated transcytosis, nanoparticle, and viral vector approaches
## 3. Neuropsychiatry
- Active interest in treatment-resistant depression, schizophrenia negative symptoms, autism
- Values novel mechanisms beyond monoamine modulation
- Interested in rapid-acting antidepressant approaches
- Seeking biomarker-defined patient populations in neuropsychiatry
## 4. AI and Computational Neuroscience
- Recursion partnership ($150M+) focused on AI-driven neuroscience drug discovery
- Interested in platforms that combine AI with functional human neural systems
- Values computational approaches that predict clinical translatability
- Open to digital biomarker and digital therapeutics partnerships
## 5. Rare Neurological Diseases
- Active in genetic neurology (SMA legacy with Spinraza)
- Interested in gene therapy approaches for monogenic CNS disorders
- Values platforms enabling rapid development across multiple rare CNS indications
- Open to AAV and non-viral gene delivery for CNS
# 5. Decision-Making Framework
When evaluating a new deal or partnership:
Scientific Lens (Co-Equal First Filter)
- Novel mechanism with strong biological rationale?
- Human translational evidence?
- Differentiated from Roche's internal programs and existing partnerships?
- Platform potential beyond lead indication?
- Biomarker strategy compatible with Roche Diagnostics?
Strategic Lens
- Fills a defined portfolio gap?
- Enhances Roche's therapeutic area competitive position?
- Creates platform value across multiple programs?
- Diagnostic integration potential?
- Strengthens Roche's innovation network?
Commercial Lens
- Significant market opportunity? (Flexible on size if science is compelling)
- Competitive differentiation durable?
- Global reimbursement pathway?
- Patient impact meaningful?
Execution Lens
- Partner capabilities and track record?
- Development plan quality and timeline?
- Regulatory pathway clarity?
- Manufacturing feasibility?
- Cultural fit for long-term collaboration?
# 6. View on Deal Structures & Partnerships
Lindqvist has strong preferences shaped by Roche's partnership culture:
Preferred Structures:
- Research collaborations with defined milestones and option-to-license
- Platform partnerships with multiple program rights
- Co-development with shared governance and Roche opt-in at key milestones
- Roche Venture Fund investments as relationship seeds (typically $5-30M)
- Technology access deals for AI, delivery, and diagnostics integration
- Selective M&A for transformative assets or platform technologies
Structures He Values:
- Joint steering committees with scientific depth
- Multi-program frameworks that expand over time
- Diagnostic co-development provisions
- Technology transfer and knowledge sharing
- Publication rights and academic engagement clauses
Structures He Avoids:
- Pure financial transactions without scientific collaboration
- Deals where Roche is just a passive licensee
- Partnerships without meaningful governance input
- Short-term arrangements without expansion potential
- Exclusive in-licensing without co-development component
Key Deal Terms He Prioritizes:
- Broad therapeutic area rights with option to expand
- Joint scientific governance (Roche scientists at the table)
- Companion diagnostic development rights
- Manufacturing technology transfer provisions
- Multi-program option structures
- Publication and academic collaboration provisions
Recent Deal Precedents Shaping His Thinking:
- Recursion ($150M+ AI neuroscience) — model for AI-platform partnership at scale
- Manifold Bio ($55M BBB delivery) — targeted technology partnership for CNS delivery
- Trontinemab development — internal Brainshuttle validates BBB delivery investment
- Sarepta collaboration model — deep neuroscience partnership structure

# 9. How He Engages with Biotech
First Meeting:
- Focuses on scientific hypothesis and mechanism differentiation
- Explores platform breadth beyond lead program
- Assesses diagnostic integration potential
- Evaluates team scientific depth
Second Meeting:
- Brings Roche therapeutic area scientists and diagnostic experts
- Deep data review and translational evidence assessment
- Discusses potential collaboration framework
- Explores multi-program partnership possibilities
Third Meeting:
- Deal structure and governance framework
- Financial terms and milestone design
- Manufacturing and technology transfer discussion
- Cultural compatibility assessment
He disengages if:
- The science is incremental without clear differentiation
- The company only wants a licensing deal without collaboration
- There's no platform value beyond the lead asset
- The partner views Roche as interchangeable with other pharma
- The team lacks scientific depth for meaningful collaboration
