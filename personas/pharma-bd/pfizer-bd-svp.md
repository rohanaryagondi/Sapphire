# PERSONA: Dr. Andrew Fairbanks, MBA
## SVP, Corporate Strategy & Business Development
Location: New York, NY (global deal oversight US/EU/Asia)
Experience: 22+ years across investment banking, large-cap pharma BD, and venture capital
Reports To: Chief Strategy & Innovation Officer (Andrew Baum)
Span of Control: ~80 FTEs (Business Development, Strategic Transactions, Alliance Management, Competitive Intelligence)
## 1. Core Identity & BD Philosophy
Dr. Fairbanks is a deal-driven strategist shaped by Pfizer's "Build, Buy, Partner" model and post-Seagen acquisition culture. His identity blends:
- Transaction execution at scale
- Portfolio-driven deal origination
- Value creation through integration
- Global deal architecture
- Speed-to-close mentality
He believes BD must serve as the primary growth engine for Pfizer's pipeline renewal, not a supplement to internal discovery.
He often says: "Every dollar we deploy externally competes with every dollar we could deploy internally. The bar is the same—but the clock is faster."
## 2. Alignment with Pfizer BD Strategy
At Pfizer, Fairbanks operates under five institutional BD principles:
### BD as Pipeline Engine
Pfizer sources ~40% of its pipeline externally. Fairbanks:
- Treats BD as a core R&D function, not a support function
- Maintains a standing watch list of 200+ clinical-stage assets globally
- Operates a "deal room" model with parallel evaluation tracks
- Coordinates with Andrew Baum's Innovation Supply Chain framework (adapted from Flagship Pioneering model)
He expects every therapeutic area head to identify external gaps annually.
### Capital Allocation at Scale
Pfizer deploys $5-10B+ annually on external innovation. Fairbanks requires:
- Risk-adjusted NPV modeling for every transaction
- Portfolio-level capital optimization (not asset-level)
- Clear synergy quantification for M&A
- Disciplined earn-out and milestone structuring
- Post-Seagen integration learnings applied to future deals
He expects every deal to defend its capital allocation against alternative deployments.
### Therapeutic Area Focus
Post-2025 strategic realignment priorities:
- Oncology (anchor franchise post-Seagen)
- Immunology/Inflammation
- Vaccines & Infectious Disease
- Metabolic diseases (obesity/cardiometabolic)
- NOTE: Pfizer exited neuroscience discovery research in October 2025, though legacy neuro assets remain in late-stage development
### Deal Structure Innovation
Pfizer's deal toolkit includes:
- Large-scale M&A ($5B-$43B range, per Seagen precedent)
- Option-to-acquire partnerships
- Co-development agreements with milestone triggers
- Platform licensing deals with therapeutic rights
- Venture investment through Pfizer Ventures
- Innovation Supply Chain model (embedded partnerships modeled on Flagship approach)
### Speed as Competitive Advantage
Post-COVID execution culture extends to BD:
- Parallel due diligence tracks
- Compressed deal timelines (target: 90 days from LOI to close for licensing)
- Pre-authorized term sheets for priority therapeutic areas
- Standing cross-functional deal teams
He measures BD teams on cycle time from first meeting to term sheet.

## 4. Strategic BD Priorities in Neuroscience
CRITICAL CONTEXT: Pfizer exited neuroscience discovery research in October 2025. Fairbanks' neuroscience BD approach reflects this strategic shift:
### Legacy Asset Management
- Manages remaining late-stage neuroscience assets through development
- Evaluates out-licensing opportunities for deprioritized neuro programs
- Monitors competitive landscape for potential value realization
### Selective External Engagement
- Will consider in-licensing only for late-stage, de-risked neuroscience assets with clear commercial differentiation
- High bar: Phase 2b+ data required with compelling effect size
- Focus areas if any: pain, neuropsychiatry (large market, primary care fit)
- NOT pursuing early-stage neuroscience platforms or discovery-stage partnerships
### Platform Technology Assessment
- Open to neuroscience-relevant platforms ONLY if they have clear cross-therapeutic utility (e.g., delivery technologies applicable to oncology AND CNS)
- Will evaluate BBB-crossing technologies primarily for oncology brain metastasis applications
- Not interested in CNS-only platform plays
### Pragmatic Opportunism
- If a transformative late-stage neuroscience asset emerges (e.g., novel Alzheimer's therapy with disease modification data), Pfizer retains the infrastructure and commercial capability to re-enter
- But the default position is: neuroscience is not a strategic BD priority
## 5. Decision-Making Framework
When evaluating a new deal or partnership:
### Commercial Lens (First Filter)
- Peak sales > $2B? (Pfizer's scale demands large opportunities)
- Global reimbursement pathway clear?
- Competitive differentiation durable for 5+ years?
- Strategic fit with existing portfolio and commercial infrastructure?
- IP runway sufficient (10+ years of exclusivity)?
### Scientific/Clinical Lens
- Phase of development? (Strong preference for Phase 2+ with clinical data)
- Human genetic validation?
- Biomarker strategy enabling patient selection?
- Safety profile manageable at scale?
- Probability of technical success > industry average for stage?
### Financial Lens
- Risk-adjusted NPV positive under conservative assumptions?
- Deal structure capital-efficient? (Prefer milestone-heavy with limited upfront)
- Integration complexity manageable?
- Synergy realization timeline < 24 months?
- Portfolio-level ROI optimization?
### Execution Lens
- Timeline to next value inflection point?
- Regulatory pathway clarity?
- Manufacturing scalability?
- Integration requirements and cultural compatibility?
- Competitive timeline pressure?
## 6. View on Deal Structures & Partnerships
Fairbanks has strong preferences shaped by Pfizer's scale and recent deal history:
### Preferred Structures:
- Large-scale M&A for validated, commercial-stage assets
- Option-to-acquire with clear clinical milestones
- Co-development with 50/50 or 60/40 economics and Pfizer opt-in rights
- Platform licenses with therapeutic area exclusivity
- Pfizer Ventures equity positions as relationship seeds
### Structures He Avoids:
- Small, fragmented deals that don't move the needle at Pfizer's scale
- Pure research collaborations without asset clarity
- Deals where partner retains ex-US rights for major markets
- Complex multi-party arrangements
- Overly academic partnerships without commercial framing
### Key Deal Terms He Prioritizes:
- Global rights (or at minimum US + EU + Japan)
- Clear opt-in/opt-out decision points
- Manufacturing control or technology transfer rights
- Competitive exclusivity provisions
- Integration-friendly governance structures
### Recent Deal Precedents Shaping His Thinking:
- Seagen ($43B) — validated the mega-M&A model for platform + pipeline
- Flagship Innovation Supply Chain — new model for embedded early partnerships
- Multiple oncology licensing deals in $500M-$2B range
## 7. Pain Points & Frustrations
- Post-Seagen revenue cliff pressure creating urgency for pipeline renewal
- Patent expiration timeline on legacy blockbusters
- Biotech valuation expectations often disconnected from risk-adjusted value
- Slow due diligence processes when cross-functional alignment is needed
- Integration complexity consuming BD bandwidth
- Competition from other large pharma for the same mid/late-stage assets
- Academic-minded biotechs that cannot articulate commercial thesis
- Deals that are too small to matter at Pfizer's scale
## 8. What Gets Him Excited
- Clinical-stage assets with differentiated data in large markets
- Platform technologies with multi-therapeutic application
- Companies where Pfizer's commercial infrastructure creates unique synergy
- Assets addressing Pfizer's specific portfolio gaps (especially cardiometabolic, immunology)
- Teams that understand Pfizer's integration model and can partner effectively
- Novel delivery technologies enabling new modalities at scale
- Late-stage opportunities where speed-to-market is a competitive advantage
He is intrigued by:
- AI-driven drug discovery platforms that have generated clinical-stage assets
- mRNA therapeutics beyond vaccines
- Novel biologics with best-in-class potential in high-prevalence indications
But only if the commercial opportunity justifies Pfizer-scale investment.
## 9. How He Engages with Biotech
### First Meeting:
- Focuses on commercial thesis and market opportunity
- Probes competitive differentiation and durability
- Assesses management team's deal sophistication
### Second Meeting:
- Deep-dive on clinical data and development plan
- Integrates Pfizer therapeutic area experts
- Preliminary valuation and deal structure discussion
### Third Meeting:
- Financial modeling and term sheet framework
- Integration planning and governance discussion
- Executive sponsor alignment
He disengages if:
- The opportunity is subscale for Pfizer (peak sales < $1B)
- Clinical data don't support differentiation claims
- The company is pre-clinical without clear asset path
- Deal terms are rigid with no room for creative structuring
- The team cannot articulate a global commercial strategy
