# Duchenne muscular dystrophy — FDA vs EMA divergence (both directions)

DMD is the cleanest case where FDA/EMA divergence runs **both ways**, and both decisions turn on the
same question: does a **biochemical surrogate** (dystrophin restoration) justify approval absent proof
of functional benefit?

## Eteplirsen (Exondys 51) — FDA-yes / EMA-no
- **FDA:** accelerated approval, Sept 2016, accepting increased dystrophin as a surrogate reasonably
  likely to predict clinical benefit (a controversial decision; two FDA reviewers dissented).
- **EMA:** **negative CHMP opinion**, 1 June 2018 (confirmed on re-examination Sept 2018) — "the
  Committee for Medicinal Products for Human Use (CHMP) of the European Medicines Agency (EMA), has
  adopted a negative opinion for EXONDYS (eteplirsen)… amenable to skipping exon 51 of the dystrophin
  gene." [Sarepta Therapeutics press release, 1 Jun 2018]
- **Divergence implication:** acceptance of a biochemical surrogate is regulator-specific; a US
  accelerated approval on a surrogate does **not** predict EMA acceptance, which demanded functional
  evidence.

## Ataluren (Translarna) — EMA-yes / FDA-no (the reverse)
- **EMA:** conditional marketing authorisation, 2014, for ambulatory nonsense-mutation DMD.
- **FDA:** **Refuse to File**, 22 Feb 2016 — "it received yesterday evening a Refuse to File letter
  from the United States Food and Drug Administration (FDA) regarding PTC's New Drug Application (NDA)
  for Translarna (ataluren)… the application was not sufficiently complete to permit a substantive
  review." [PTC Therapeutics press release, 23 Feb 2016] The FDA never approved it.
- **Divergence implication:** divergence runs both ways — a drug can be EU-authorised yet fail even to
  be *filed* by the FDA. EU conditional-approval precedent does not lower the US filing/substantial-
  evidence bar.

## The surrogate at the centre — the biomedical basis (EMET pass)
Why both decisions hinge on dystrophin: the surrogate is **real but modest and variable**, and its link
to function was unproven.
- Systemic eteplirsen (AVI-4658): "induced exon 51 skipping in all cohorts and new dystrophin protein
  expression in a significant dose-dependent (p=0.0203), but variable, manner" — in responders, mean
  dystrophin rose from ~8.9% to 16.4% of normal control. [Cirak et al. 2011, *Lancet*, PMID 21784508 —
  via EMET] Earlier intramuscular proof-of-concept showed local restoration to a mean 26.4% of healthy
  control. [Kinali et al. 2009, *Lancet Neurol*, PMID 19713152]
- This quantifies exactly what the FDA accepted and the EMA rejected: a small biochemical gain whose
  clinical meaning was contested.
