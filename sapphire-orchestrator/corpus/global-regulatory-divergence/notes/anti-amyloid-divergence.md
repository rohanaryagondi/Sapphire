# Anti-amyloid Alzheimer's mAbs — FDA vs ex-US divergence

The amyloid-β monoclonal antibodies are the clearest recent CNS case where the FDA and ex-US bodies
split on the **same trials**. Three layers of divergence: (1) regulator approval, (2) genotype-gated
label scope, (3) HTA/reimbursement.

## Aducanumab (Aduhelm) — the flagship refusal
- **FDA:** accelerated approval, June 2021, on amyloid lowering as a surrogate "reasonably likely" to
  predict benefit.
- **EMA:** **refused** — "On 17 December 2021, the European Medicines Agency (EMA)… decided not to
  authorise aducanumab for the treatment of mild cognitive impairment due to Alzheimer's disease and
  mild Alzheimer's dementia." Biogen withdrew on re-examination (April 2022).
  [Alzheimer Europe, 17 Dec 2021 — reporting the EMA decision]
- **Divergence implication:** a surrogate-based US accelerated approval can be wholly rejected ex-US.
  EMA required demonstrated clinical benefit, not amyloid reduction alone.

## Lecanemab (Leqembi) & donanemab (Kisunla) — approved ex-US, but narrower and unfunded
- **MHRA (regulator):** licensed **both** for early AD, but **restricted to ApoE4 non-carriers and
  heterozygotes** (excluding ε4 homozygotes):
  - lecanemab, 22 Aug 2024 — "Lecanemab is approved to treat adults in the early stages of Alzheimer's
    disease who have one or no copies of the apolipoprotein E4 gene (ApoE4)." [MHRA, gov.uk]
  - donanemab, 23 Oct 2024 — same ApoE4 restriction. [MHRA, gov.uk]
  This is **narrower than the FDA's initial label**, which carried no upfront genotype gate.
- **NICE (HTA):** **did not recommend** either drug for the NHS (final draft guidance, 19 Jun 2025):
  "neither drug can be recommended for treating mild cognitive impairment or mild dementia caused by
  Alzheimer's disease" — benefit too small for the cost. [NICE]
- **Divergence implication:** marketing authorisation ≠ market access. An anti-amyloid programme can
  clear an ex-US regulator (MHRA) yet be **blocked at the HTA/reimbursement gate** (NICE) — the binding
  commercial constraint in single-payer systems — while the genotype restriction further narrows the
  addressable population vs the US.

## Why the genotype gate? — the biomedical basis (EMET pass)
The ApoE4 restriction tracks a steep **APOE ε4 allele-dose effect on ARIA**:
- **Lecanemab:** ARIA-E "more common in ApoE e4 carriers (16.8%) and most common in ApoE ε4 homozygous
  participants (34.5%)." [Honig et al. 2024, *Alzheimers Res Ther*, PMID 38730496 — via EMET]
- **Donanemab:** ARIA-E 24.4% vs placebo 1.9% (ARIA-H 31.3% vs 13.0%), with "independent associations
  between ARIA-E and … increased risk with APOE ε4 allele number." [Zimmer/Mintun et al. 2025,
  *JAMA Neurol*, PMID 40063015 — via EMET]

This is the C1–C2 mechanism *behind* the regulatory decisions: regulators outside the US used
genotype-stratified ARIA risk to gate the label where the FDA initially did not.
