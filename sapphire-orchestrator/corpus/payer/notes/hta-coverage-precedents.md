# Notes — HTA & Coverage Precedents for CNS Drugs

**Theme:** Cost-effectiveness assessments and Medicare coverage determinations for CNS drug classes. These precedents define the reimbursement environment that any new CNS program will enter.

---

## 1. Anti-Amyloid Alzheimer's Disease mAbs (lecanemab / aducanumab)

### ICER Assessment (April 2023) [T2]
ICER's California Technology Assessment Forum (CTAF) voted 12-3 that currently available evidence is "not adequate" to demonstrate a net health benefit for lecanemab over supportive care. The independent panel unanimously (15-0) rated the drug's long-term value as "low" at the list price of $26,500/year.

ICER's Health-Benefit Price Benchmark: **$8,900–$21,500/year** (vs. $26,500 list).

Source: ICER Final Evidence Report, April 2023. URL: `https://icer.org/news-insights/press-releases/icer-publishes-final-evidence-report-on-lecanemab-for-alzheimers-disease/`

### CMS NCD 200.3 (April 2022 → expanded July 2023) [T1]
CMS issued NCD 200.3 requiring **Coverage with Evidence Development (CED)** for all anti-amyloid mAbs. Key terms:
- Accelerated approval (surrogate endpoint) → RCT enrollment only.
- Traditional approval (direct clinical benefit) → broader CED in CMS-approved registries.
- Patients: MCI or mild AD dementia + confirmed amyloid plaque on brain; physician must participate in qualifying registry.

After lecanemab received traditional FDA approval (July 6, 2023), CMS expanded coverage under CED — but registry enrollment remains required. Patients pay 20% Part B coinsurance.

**Precedent implication:** Any CNS drug class where FDA approves on a surrogate endpoint should expect Medicare CED as the coverage floor; traditional approval opens CED access but does NOT equal unrestricted reimbursement. This creates real-world uptake friction and limits the addressable commercial market until registry evidence accumulates.

## 2. SMA Gene Therapy and ASO Pricing Precedent (nusinersen, onasemnogene)

### ICER SMA Assessment (March 2019) [T2]
ICER assessed both nusinersen (Spinraza®, Biogen) and onasemnogene abeparvovec (Zolgensma®, Novartis/AveXis) for spinal muscular atrophy.

- **Spinraza:** List price $750,000 year 1 / $375,000 annually. ICER voted unanimously "low" long-term value. Value-based range: $72,000–$130,000 year 1 / $36,000–$65,000 annually.
- **Zolgensma:** Price unknown at assessment. Cost-effective range: $310,000–$900,000 per treatment.

ICER recommendations included outcome-based contracts for both products.

**Precedent implication:** RNA/ASO and gene therapy drugs for rare CNS diseases face extreme "low-value" verdicts at initial pricing. Outcome-based risk-sharing with payers is essentially required to maintain formulary access. CNS programs targeting rare indications (small N, high list price) should budget for payer negotiations pre-launch.

## 3. CNS Addiction/Dependency Drug Pricing (opioid use disorder)

### ICER OUD ER Assessment (October 2018) [T2]
ICER found extended-release buprenorphine (Probuphine, implant) and naltrexone (Vivitrol, ER injection) provided only marginal clinical benefits over generic buprenorphine/naloxone at significantly higher costs. Probuphine's list price exceeded cost-effectiveness thresholds; a 53–65% discount was needed.

**Precedent implication:** When a branded CNS addiction drug competes against a generic-equivalent standard of care, payers apply cost-minimization logic. Premium pricing requires RCT evidence of superior outcomes vs. the generic comparator — adherence convenience alone is insufficient.

## Pattern Summary

| CNS Program Type | Payer Precedent | Key Risk |
|---|---|---|
| CNS drug with surrogate-endpoint FDA approval | CMS CED (NCD 200.3 pattern) | No full Medicare access; registry enrollment required |
| Anti-amyloid / mechanism-based CNS mAb | ICER "low value" at list price | Payer resistance; coverage restrictions |
| Gene therapy / ASO for rare neurological disease | ICER "low value"; outcome-based contracts | Severe pricing pressure; outcomes-based contracts essentially required |
| Branded CNS with generic equivalent | ICER cost-minimization | Must demonstrate superior clinical outcomes vs. generic; price premium unjustifiable |
