# What I Wish Every CNS Biotech Knew Before Their First ASO IND

I spent a long time on the review side, and the single most common avoidable
mistake I saw with antisense oligonucleotide (ASO) programs was treating the
nonclinical safety package like a small-molecule package. It is not the same.

For an intrathecally-administered CNS ASO, the FDA will expect, at minimum, a
chronic toxicology study in two species, and for CNS delivery at least one of
those should be a non-human primate because rodent intrathecal anatomy is a poor
predictor of human distribution. Plan for the NHP study to be the long pole in
your timeline — it usually is.

Histopathology of the full neuraxis matters enormously. Reviewers look hard for
neuronal vacuolation and for dorsal root ganglion (DRG) toxicity, which has been
a recurring class finding for some chemistries. If you do not pre-specify DRG
sampling and grading, you will get an information request and lose a review cycle.

A few other things that reliably come up:

- Local tolerance at the injection site, and CSF parameters over the dosing period.
- A clear justification of the starting clinical dose, ideally anchored to a
  no-observed-adverse-effect level (NOAEL) with an appropriate safety factor.
- Hybridization-based off-target analysis. The agency increasingly expects a
  bioinformatic off-target assessment, not just the on-target potency story.

On clinical trial design: for a first-in-human CNS ASO, single ascending dose
into a small, well-characterized cohort is the norm, with conservative dose
escalation and pre-defined stopping rules tied to CSF and neuro exam findings.

If I had to summarize: the IND lives or dies on the NHP chronic tox study and the
neuraxis histopathology. Get those right and most other gaps are survivable.
