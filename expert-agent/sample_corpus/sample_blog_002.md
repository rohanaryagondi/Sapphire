# Designing First-in-Human CNS Trials Without Getting Burned

People obsess over the molecule and underinvest in the trial design. For CNS
programs the design choices you make at IND essentially lock in your speed for
the next two years.

My rules of thumb for a first-in-human (FIH) CNS study:

1. Start with single ascending dose (SAD), then multiple ascending dose (MAD).
   Do not try to be clever and combine them in the first protocol.
2. Build in a sentinel-dosing scheme: dose one or two subjects per cohort first,
   review safety, then dose the rest. For CNS this is not optional in my view.
3. Pre-specify stopping rules. Vague "investigator judgment" language gets
   pushback and slows your review.
4. Bake in biomarkers / target engagement early. A CSF pharmacodynamic readout
   that shows you are hitting the target is worth more than another tox cohort
   when you go to raise money.

On the safety-study question that everyone asks about IND timing: the clinical
hold risk for CNS programs clusters around (a) inadequate chronic toxicology,
(b) DRG and neuraxis histopathology gaps, and (c) an unjustified starting dose.
If your nonclinical package addresses those three, you are usually in decent
shape to open a Phase 1.

One opinion I will flag as opinion, not established fact: I think adaptive dose
escalation designs (e.g., Bayesian) are underused in CNS Phase 1 and would
de-risk these programs, but reviewers are still conservative about them, so
budget extra time for the conversation if you go that route.
